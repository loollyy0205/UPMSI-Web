By. Mia N. Judicpa & Gabrielle A. Abrahan
April 1, 2022

Around four months a year, we experience a respite from our tropical climate when the weather starts to chill. During Christmas, we wear thick clothes as winds from Siberia and China descend upon our mornings and bring with them good — and cold — tidings.

These winds prevail during the northeast monsoon season, which we know locally as Amihan. It is one of the two seasons our country experiences annually. They visit us from November to late March, and are followed by the winds that blow during the southeast monsoon season, called Habagat, after the transition months of April–May.

How do these winds interact with our waters and islands? What else do these winds bring?

Weather in the ocean

The winds interact with the ocean to generate currents that redistribute heat around the world, thereby regulating and stabilizing climate patterns. Ocean currents may also be driven by the earth’s rotation, the tides, and by differences in water density.

If you zoom out, you will see large circular ocean currents formed by global wind patterns and forces created by Earth’s rotation called gyres. Five major gyres occupy the earth’s oceans: the North and South Pacific Subtropical Gyres, the North and South Atlantic Subtropical Gyres, and the Indian Ocean Subtropical Gyre. Aside from the larger and more permanent gyres, there are many types of currents: eddies, whirlpools, deep ocean currents, and jet streams.1

Eddies at the back of an island form when stronger winds interact with the rugged terrain of the island or an oceanic flow passes the island. They are vortex-like phenomena that can last for months, and may be as small as a couple of centimeters or as large as hundreds of kilometers across. The swirling motion of eddies causes nutrients normally found in colder, deeper waters to rise to the surface. With enough light for photosynthesis to occur, this process influences the biological productivity of the area.2-5

Mesoscale (large-scale, less than 100 km) eddies are frequently observed in the seas around and within the Philippine archipelago. The multitude of embayments and isolated deep seas within a network of interconnecting straits, coupled with the complex bathymetry of the ocean floor and the rugged topography of the Philippines, all contribute to the formation of eddies.

Many eddies, represented by these swirling patterns, form around the world. They are part of a large current system that transports nutrients and influences biological productivity.

Curiosities in Panay Strait

Dr. Charina Lyn Amedo-Repollo was born on Panay Island, the westernmost island in the Visayas. She tells us how her work in physical oceanography began right in her hometown:

“When I [returned to research], a professor from the University of Hawai’i was looking for students to be trained in handling and operating the high-frequency (HF) radars. There was a big program in collaboration with UP MSI, the Philippine Strait Dynamics Experiment. Since he was looking for a student to train, I was recommended by Dr. [Cesar] Villanoy because he knows I was born and raised in Panay. This helped me a lot in selecting the sites, managing the logistics, installing, operating, and maintaining the HF radars.”

One of the sites for the HF radar setup was located in the municipality of Laua-an, Antique. Boasting a productive maritime economy, Panay is bordered by the Iloilo and Guimaras Straits, Panay Gulf, and the Visayan, Sibuyan, and Sulu Seas.6 The topography of the island is dominated by mountain ranges and hilly areas on either side.

The Panay Strait is bounded by the coast of Panay Island on the east and the Palawan Island chain on the west. With the surrounding Semirara Sea, Panay shelf, Cuyo shelf, and Cuyo Group of Islands, the Panay Strait is a topographically intricate system consisting of shelves, small low-lying islands, seamounts, a deep basin, and sills forming the shallowest point within the strait.

Panay Island, the westernmost island in central Philippines, is surrounded by many bodies of water that hold a rich marine ecosystem.

Many oceanographic processes control the water flow here, including tides, sea level variations between the South China Sea and the Pacific Ocean, changing of the monsoon seasons, the El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO), and the episodic occurrence of tropical cyclones.7-11

The many features within and surrounding Panay Strait make it an ideal location for eddies to form.

The publication,Low-frequency surface currents and generation of an island lee eddy in Panay Island, Philippines12, describes water flow and movement in Panay Strait, identifying prevalent eddies and jet flows. Intensive observations from the cruise and satellite images support the findings that there are regions in Panay Strait that are productive biological hubs.

Using remote sensing to map water movements

Dr. Repollo and her colleagues used a specialized shore-based remote sensing system with over-the-horizon (OTH) radar technology to monitor ocean surface currents. They were able to detect eddy formations that generate upwelling. Upwelling is an oceanographic process in which currents bring cooler, and usually nutrient-rich, deep water towards the ocean surface. These nutrients “fertilize” surface waters, thus often have high biological productivity and therefore, good fishing grounds.

With HF radars deployed in three points (Pandan, Laua-an and Tobias Fornier) along the coastline of Antique, they characterized the spatial and temporal variability of surface currents. They also used Acoustic Doppler Current Profiler (ADCP) data to describe the three-dimensional circulation in the area.

Phytoplankton blooms are indicators of marine ecosystem health. Correlating with the idea that eddy sites have high biological productivity, chlorophyll (a photosynthetic pigment present in all algal species) levels can be used as a proxy for phytoplankton biomass and indicate upwelling. Satellite ocean color technology is widely used to detect, map, and monitor chlorophyll. In the satellite images, the greener the color, the higher the chlorophyll concentration.

Local circulations

From the HF Radar data and chlorophyll-a level estimates, Dr. Repollo was able to identify two dominant phenomena in Panay Strait: the seasonal cyclonic Panay Eddy and its intensified eastern flow, the Panay Coastal Jet. She also described how monsoon patterns in the Philippines influence Panay Eddy formation.

The Philippines experiences the northeast monsoon winds from November to March, and the southwest monsoon winds from June to September.

The Philippine archipelago contains many isolated circulations that are unique within the area. These local circulations are partly influenced by the seasonal reversal of the monsoon winds that the country experiences: the southwest monsoon from June to September and the northeast monsoon from November to March. The topographies of the ocean floor and of the surrounding land masses play a significant part as well.

The formation of Panay Eddy is due to the stronger and relatively stable northeast monsoon winds. As these winds blow across the mountainous islands of Mindoro and Panay, they intensify between islands and weaken at the lee or back of the island. This causes the winds to rotate counterclockwise (cyclonic) and generates upwelling.

Upwelling brings cold, nutrient rich water from the depths of the ocean to the surface, which is important for primary productivity (algal growth). This, in turn, supports a richly productive marine ecosystem and is the reason for the abundant fisheries and sightings of large marine mammals in this region.

The Panay Eddy starts to form in November, peaks in February, and decays by late March. It is 100 km across in size, with an effective depth of about 130 m. Depending on the strength and direction of the prevailing Amihan, the center of the eddy may shift across the strait.

Linking physical oceanography and fisheries

If there is one huge difference between terrestrial and marine environments, it is that the latter constantly moves, impacting the supply of nutrients, sometimes enhancing the ocean’s productivity and, ultimately, the food web.

Dr. Repollo’s background in fisheries helped her establish the significant links between physical processes in the ocean and marine productivity.

“Actually, there are a lot of processes between that link. It takes time for you to be able to predict [implications of physical phenomena] into fisheries. But there are studies, including ours, that really link these processes [together]. When you observe a similar physical feature, usually, it is also high in biological production. Anywhere you go, upwelling areas will always be productive — they are known to be major fishing grounds.

Many structures perished during World War II, including the Bureau of Science Herbarium.1 It was the first repository of plants in the country, established in 1908 by botanist and taxonomist Dr. Elmer Merrill. The herbarium housed more than a million specimens, some dating back to the 1700s. The fire took down the building and decades’ worth of field collection, preservation, and species identification.

The herbarium, which was renamed to Philippine National Herbarium during the latter years, recovered with the help of other herbaria in Europe and the United States. Duplicates of the lost specimens were returned, as overseen by biologist Dr. Eduardo Quisumbing. Still, the loss of the largest collection at the time in Southeast Asia is a reminder of how integral herbaria are to the study of plants and related organisms, especially in a country with high biodiversity.

The Philippines, a center of biodiversity

The United Nations Environment Programme identifies the Philippines as one of 18 mega-biodiverse countries. Its grasslands, tropical rainforests, freshwater and estuarine ecosystems, and open water contain two-thirds of the global biodiversity, with 70–80% of the world’s plant and animal species.2

On land, the Amazon Forest holds the highest density of floral and faunal species. In water, however, that feat belongs to the Coral Triangle. The area of 6 million km2, includes the sea zones of the Philippines, the Solomon Islands, Indonesia, Malaysia, Papua New Guinea, and East Timor.

The Philippines is home to 5% of the world’s flora and has the 5th highest number of plant species. Located within the Coral Triangle of the western Pacific Ocean, the marine biodiversity of this archipelago consists of 468 hard corals, 1755 reef fishes, 820 species of algae, and 19 species of seagrass.3

Threats to this rich biodiversity come in both natural and anthropogenic forms. Specifically, pollution, climate change, unsustainable culture practices and destructive fishing methods, eutrophication from surrounding mariculture sites, and even coastal development are all possible causes of biodiversity loss.4

To protect the biodiversity of our macroalgae, scientists and collectors comb sandy shorelines, rocky beaches, and the open water to collect seaweed specimens and keep them in an herbarium. Originally a term coined by French botanist Joseph Pitton de Tournefort to refer to a book about medicinal plants,5 herbarium now refers to a collection of dried and preserved plant specimens for scientific research.

The Gregorio T. Velasquez Phycological Herbarium (MSI) of the UP Marine Science Institute, of which Dr. Santiañez acts as curator, was designed to house the seaweeds of the Philippines.

Protecting Philippine phycology

Seaweeds — or “benthic marine macroalgae,” if you want to sound technical — play multiple roles in the ocean as primary producers, habitat-forming organisms, and nutrient regulators.6 They are marine plants that photosynthesize and provide food for several fishes. Some species of seaweeds, like the brown seaweed Sargassum, provide shelter to fish and other marine animals by forming large mats that are buoyed by gas-filled cavities called pneumatocysts. For instance, the siganids or rabbitfishes (locally known as danggit) settle, breed, and feed in the Sargassum habitats of the productive Lagonoy Gulf. Siganids are economically important in the Philippines, as they make up around 500,000 kg of the total fisheries production annually.7

Seaweeds are emerging assets in aquaculture. They are food sources, as well as alternative means for fishermen and coastal communities to earn. In Northern Philippines, the gamet (Phycocalidia) harvesters of Ilocos Norte carefully handpick the edible seaweed from sharp, slippery rocks.8 They sell it as a dried product pressed into a disc structure that the locals call pedazo, with prices reaching PhP 500 per square foot.9

Protecting seaweeds involves comprehensive research on their diversity, biology, and ecology, among others. By cataloging, archiving, and providing researchers easy access to specimens, herbaria play a big role in this field of research. This is why Dr. Gavino C. Trono, Jr. established the Gregorio T. Velasquez Phycological Herbarium.

With the aid of some seed money and Ms. Corazon Dayrit, the first in-house personnel of the herbarium, Dr. Trono was able to amass quite a number of specimens. The collection comprised donations from Dr. Velasquez and Dr. Miguel D. Fortes, items from herbaria exchanges, materials collected by Dr. Trono and his students, and specimens from different UP MSI expeditions.

The G.T. Velasquez Phycological Herbarium (MSI)

The collections of the G.T. Velasquez Phycological Herbarium (MSI) are contained in two air conditioned rooms, each with an area of 40 m2 (approx. 431 ft2). Dried voucher specimens are stored in one cabinet type, while the other type was modified to accommodate crustose coralline algae and bulky seaweeds. The rooms have fixed working counters where students and researchers can prepare or study specimens.

Building a better understanding of seaweeds across the Philippines

Seaweeds and seagrasses are modest inhabitants of the ocean; they don’t garner as much attention as other ocean-dwellers. However, Dr. Santiañez believes that developing the Herbarium can help people appreciate them more. While the MSI Herbarium is equipped with the appropriate tools and fixtures, Dr. Santiañez emphasizes that more additions are needed. Manpower is at the top of the list:

“I would really want [more staff]. I think may person na talaga na mas-angkop yung training for natural history collections. I’ve been attending some of the trainings recently pero iba pa rin talaga like may course na museology para sa pag-manage ng collections.”

(“I would really want [more staff]. I think there is someone whose training is definitely more suited for natural history collections. I’ve been attending some of the trainings recently, but having someone who graduated with a degree in Museology to manage the collections is a different story.”)

As a facility for research, teaching, and training, an herbarium should include features that expedite the search for information, including the digitization of the collections, which was already initiated by Dr. Ganzon-Fortes during her time. If provided with sufficient funding, Dr. Santiañez plans to migrate the databases to contemporary software as part of the facilities upgrade.

Another way to achieve this is to connect herbaria located around the country to create a centralized system for specimens.

“I also want the herbarium to be a hub for all the phycological herbaria in the Philippines, so there’s a consortium with different herbaria. MSI will be the center of everything. I think the phycological herbaria usually have seagrass and mangroves as well,” Dr. Santiañez explains.

The Gregorio T. Velasquez Phycological Herbarium (MSI) highlights and showcases the Philippine collection of seaweeds and seagrasses to local and foreign visitors, from scientists to other stakeholders. Herbaria are essential to teaching and training, especially to biodiversity systematics research and ecology conservation.

“It’s not just about research. Pwede din kasi siyang ma-consider as national heritage and pride ng country kasi you have documentation na you have all these resources.”

(“It’s not just about research. It can also be considered as a national heritage and source of pride because you have documentation that you have all these resources.”)

Curious about the collections?


The G.T. Velasquez Phycological Herbarium serves many purposes: a gallery for Philippine seaweeds and seagrasses, a repository for type and voucher specimens, and a tool for scientific pursuits.

Interested students and researchers can access the collections by emailing the MSI Herbarium Collections Manager at msiadmin@msi.upd.edu.ph and/or herbarium@msi.upd.edu.ph.

Access to and use of the herbarium, its collections, and facilities are all subject to the MSI Collections Access Regulations.
