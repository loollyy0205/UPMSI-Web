By. Mia N. Judicpa & Gabrielle A. Abrahan
February 4, 2022

Good bacteria roam the human skin freely, forming an additional protective layer against pathogens. They make up a part of the human microbiota: a collection of microorganisms that inhabit our body and contribute their own functions for our well-being. Together, the human host and the microbiota are known as the “holobiont.”

Like us, corals host a myriad of microorganisms. Loosely, their microbiome consists of photosynthetic algae, fungi, archaea, prokaryotic bacteria, and even viruses. While our microbiome mostly functions as additional protection against pathogenic microbes, the coral microbiome actually participates in many life processes, from metabolism to supplying essential amino acids. More and more studies have proven how integral the coral microbiome is to the survival of coral reefs and, consequently, of the entire marine ecosystem.

Corals are vulnerable to many stressors, most of which arise from human activity like coastal construction work, improper waste disposal, unethical fishing practices, and coral harvesting.1 Artificial light at night (ALAN) is part of this long list and has been shown to disrupt biological processes that are essential to coral growth and propagation.2-5

Disturbance in the deep

The demand for commercial and recreational sites in beachfronts and coastal towns drives many urbanization projects in these areas. With around 2.5 billion people living within 100 km of the world’s coastal areas,6 the use of electric lights like LEDs keeps skyrocketing. In fact, 22% of the world’s shorelines are illuminated by ALAN — with Asia contributing a third of that estimate — as reported in 2010.7 Smith and co-authors found that ALAN affects 1.9 million km2 of the world’s shallow waters (1 m depth).8

Studies have shown that nightly exposure to LEDs results in two primary adverse effects in corals: one, the symbiotic algae of the coral microbiome fails to efficiently perform photosynthesis and, two, the gametogenesis cycle loses synchronization.2-5 The higher-energy blue-white LEDs have a more pronounced effect than yellow LEDs.3

Now, just as ALAN has been shown to have long-term negative effects on humans, the question goes: what exactly happens in corals during these periods of change? Jake Baquiran and his colleagues worked with scientists from the Bar-Ilan University in Israel to investigate the ALAN phenomenon from multiple perspectives, including coral spawning, coral microbial communities, and coral host gene expression. The 2020 paper, “The Prokaryotic Microbiome of Acropora digitifera is Stable under Short-Term Artificial Light Pollution,”9 posits that ALAN can cause the coral microbiome to change.

The group used gravid (pregnant) specimens of Acropora digitifera, subjecting them to three light treatments over a two-month period. Sequencing of these corals revealed that their microbial communities did not change much with ALAN. Even then, other interesting patterns were observed.

Baquiran and the team dove into the Caniogan Reef of the Bolinao-Anda Reef Complex (Pangasinan, Northwestern Philippines) to collect 45 colonies of A. digitifera. These colonies were allowed to adjust to their new environment for 21 days, then segregated into three tanks, each subjected to a different light treatment every night: cold (bluish) white LED, warm (yellowish) white LED, and ambient light treatment (as the control group).

The A. digitifera microbiome under ALAN

Coral fragments were retrieved from select colonies at different time intervals then sent out for gene sequencing. The group found that the A. digitifera microbiome is dominated by the phyla Proteobacteria, Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Actinobacteria, Planctomycetes, Verrucomicrobia, and Cyanobacteria. Regardless of the light treatment and exposure time, the populations of these microorganisms varied across individual coral colonies.

The microbiome was stable under ALAN exposure, but changed through time.


Seeing how the microbiome composition of A. digitifera did not change significantly when exposed to warm or cold white light at night, it can be said that the coral microbiome can survive under light stress.10,11 Baquiran ascribes this to the physiological plasticity of the microbiome.12,13 Physiological plasticity is the phenomenon by which organisms — in this case, the microbial taxa that make up the A. digitifera microbiome — alter their observable traits so they can adapt better to environmental changes. By displaying plasticity, the coral microbiome can be stable even under stressful conditions, and will be able to continue to help the coral host survive.

Some microbes grow faster under ALAN exposure.


The corals subjected to both types of light treatment displayed more counts of certain members of Proteobacteria called the aerobic anoxygenic photoheterotrophic bacteria (AAPB), compared to other microorganisms. As bacteria that harness light to produce energy, AAPB activity increases under illumination, and their populations grow faster. AAPB have a large impact on the carbon cycle: since they use solar energy to survive and not complex carbon compounds like other bacteria, more carbon remains in the ocean.

There are more nitrogen-fixing microorganisms in ALAN-exposed corals.


Symbiotic algae in corals are closely associated with the microorganisms that gather nitrogen from the air and release it to the coral host in a process called nitrogen-fixing. The coral colonies that were exposed to ALAN bore more nitrogen-fixing microorganisms, also known as diazotrophs, in their microbiomes. Presence of more diazotrophs means more nitrogen can be converted into useful compounds, like ammonia, which help the symbiotic algae in the coral to grow, compounds like ammonia14, which is important to the growth of primary producers (e.g., algae).

More Endozoicomonadaceae emerged as the spawning period approached.


“The [most] interesting part of the paper is 'yung nakita namin na, as the spawning period approached, the number or the relative abundance of Endozoicomonas increased. I think 'yun ang pinaka-highlight ng paper.”

(“The [most] interesting part of the paper is that we saw, as the spawning period approached, the number or the relative abundance of Endozoicomonas increased. I think that was the top highlight of the paper.”)

Baquiran observed that the relative abundance of Endozoicomonas grew as the gravid (pregnant) coral colonies produced gametes. He and fellow writers hypothesize that Endozoicomonas may protect the gametes spawned into the water column from unwanted microbes by producing antimicrobial compounds.

Endozoicomonas use quorum sensing as a way to communicate with each other and decide on how to modify their gene expression according to how many bacterial cells are present in the environment.15 Through quorum sensing, microbes can control their physiological activities, such as antibiotic production, symbiosis, competence, and virulence.

Enlightening Insights

As we continue to see an increase in both the reach and intensity of ALAN, we need to get a better idea of how it affects or endangers corals at the micro- and macroscale. After their investigation of the ALAN-exposed coral microbiome, Baquiran and colleagues discovered that despite short-term exposure to light pollution every night, the overall coral microbiome community remained resilient, keeping the corals healthy. But this is just one aspect of the ALAN phenomenon. Baquiran shares:

“Actually, there are three papers na manggagaling sa large study na ‘to. This microbiome study, ay part lang siya ng [series]... Parang side experiment lang siya. Ang totoong focus is to look at the effect of ALAN on coral gametogenesis and spawning. So, actually, may isang paper na talaga na na-publish [...] sa Current Biology, ‘yung paper led by Dr. [Inbal] Ayalon from Israel [...] ‘Yung isa pang lalabas na paper is ‘yung study ng isa sa mga students ni Dr. Conaco na [tungkol sa] gene expression of corals affected by artificial light pollution.”

(“Actually, there are three papers that will come from this large study. This microbiome study is just one part of the [series]... It’s like a side experiment. The real focus is to look at the effect of ALAN on coral gametogenesis and spawning. So, actually, there has already been one paper published [...] in Current Biology, the paper led by Dr. [Inbal] Ayalon from Israel [...] The other paper that will be released is the study of one of Dr. Conaco’s students about the gene expression of corals affected by artificial light pollution.”

“Second is the effect of ALAN on the coral gametogenesis and spawning of the corals,” Baquiran explains. “Then, the third one na ginagawa palang sa ngayon ay ‘yung effect ng ALAN sa gene expression ng coral host and the symbionts.”

(“Then, the third one, which is currently being written, is the effect of ALAN in the gene expression of the coral host and the symbionts.”)

The findings to the second topic have been published here, while the mysteries surrounding the effect of ALAN on the gene expression of the coral host and its symbionts are yet to be uncovered.

Baquiran emphasizes that this study gives preliminary insight on the effect of ALAN on the coral microbiome, and that the effect of prolonged ALAN exposure should be examined in the future. While the initial hypothesis focused on the variability of the microbiome as a direct response to ALAN, the group obtained other interesting results, including which microbial taxa have grown and what tthis implies for the health of the coral host.

There is so much more to learn in this field of study. Future discoveries or knowledge on the possible effects of light pollution on coral reefs can greatly help LGUs and administrative bodies governing coastal towns or communities develop regulations for construction and development projects.
